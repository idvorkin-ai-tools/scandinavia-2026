DENMARK · EMPIRE

Denmark ran a slave-and-sugar empire — from the Caribbean to the Gold Coast to India.

Tidy, bicycle-friendly Denmark was, for two centuries, a colonial power. It shipped roughly a hundred thousand enslaved Africans across the Atlantic, grew sugar on Caribbean islands worked by the enslaved, held trading towns in India, and colonised Greenland — which is still part of the Kingdom today, with its own unfinished reckonings. The design museums and the slave forts belong to the same country. Hold both.

TL;DR — Under the Danish–Norwegian crown, Denmark built a small but real empire: sugar colonies in the Caribbean worked by enslaved Africans carried on Danish ships from forts on the Gold Coast; trading enclaves in India; and Greenland. Enslaved on Danish ships
~120,000

The common Danish figure for Africans carried on Danish ships over ~340 voyages. Recent voyage-by-voyage data (SlaveVoyages) records roughly 111,000 disembarked; older, incomplete counts ran near 50,000. Scholarly range ≈ 85,000–120,000.

Slave trade banned
1792

Christian VII's ordinance of 16 March 1792 made Denmark the first European state to ban the Atlantic slave trade — but it took effect only in 1803, after a decade to "stock up," and slavery itself continued to 1848.

Sold to the USA
1917

Denmark sold the West Indies to the United States for $25 million in gold; the U.S. took over on 31 March 1917 and renamed them the U.S. Virgin Islands.

One small kingdom, four corners of the world

Denmark is tiny, but its empire was not local. From Copenhagen the crown chartered companies that seized sugar islands in the Caribbean, held slave forts on the West African coast, ran trading towns in India, and planted a colony in Greenland. Tap a marker.

The one-minute mental model

1. Small country, real empire.

Denmark–Norway was never Britain or Spain, but it was a genuine colonial and slave-trading power for roughly 250 years. "Too small to matter" is a comfortable myth, not the record.

2. Sugar was the engine; slavery was the fuel.

The Caribbean islands existed to grow sugar, and sugar was grown by enslaved Africans bought at Danish forts in West Africa and shipped across the Atlantic. The whole thing was one triangular machine.

3. "First to ban the trade" is true — and not the whole truth.

Denmark did abolish the slave trade first, in 1792. But it kept enslaving people on the islands until 1848, and only freed them when the enslaved rose up. Firsts and cruelty sit side by side.

Read it as one system: the fort at Accra, the sugar mill on St. Croix, the ledger in Copenhagen, and the dividend that helped build the capital were not separate stories. They were links in a single chain.

The Danish West Indies: sugar, slavery, and 1848

From 1672 to 1917 Denmark held three Caribbean islands — St. Thomas, St. John (St. Jan), and St. Croix — as the Danish West Indies. St. Thomas became a busy free port; St. Croix became sugar country. The plantations were worked by enslaved Africans under a brutal labour regime, and the wealth flowed back to Copenhagen.

How the islands were assembled

The Danish West India Company, chartered in 1671, colonised uninhabited St. Thomas in 1672, annexed St. John in 1718, and in 1733 bought St. Croix from France (from Louis XIV) to expand sugar production. Enslaved labour did the rest — clearing, planting, cutting, and boiling cane.

The uprising that forced freedom (1848)

On the night of 2 July 1848, thousands of enslaved people gathered at Fort Frederik in Frederiksted, St. Croix — some 8,000 by morning — demanding freedom. A free or freed Black cooper remembered as "General Buddhoe" (recorded as Moses "Buddhoe" Gottlieb; some records give the name as John Gottlieb) emerged as a leader. On 3 July, Governor-General Peter von Scholten declared all enslaved people in the Danish West Indies free — acting under duress and beyond his authority.

Freedom, then a new cage: von Scholten was recalled to Denmark, tried in 1852 for exceeding his mandate, convicted, and only acquitted in 1854 shortly before his death. Meanwhile emancipation did not end coercion — a harsh labour ordinance bound the freed people to the plantations, and in 1878 the great "Fireburn" labour revolt, led by four women remembered as the "Queens" (Mary Thomas / "Queen Mary" among them), burned much of St. Croix. Freedom on paper was not freedom in the cane fields.

The slave trade and the Gold Coast forts

The enslaved people on the islands were carried there on Danish ships from forts Denmark held on the Gold Coast (today's Ghana). The chief post was Christiansborg — Osu Castle, in Accra — capital of the Danish Gold Coast, where captives were held before the Middle Passage.

Christiansborg at Accra

Danes obtained the Osu site in 1661 and made Christiansborg the headquarters of the Danish Gold Coast. Guns, liquor, cloth, iron, and beads were exchanged for gold, ivory — and human beings — who were then shipped west to the Caribbean.

How many people

Danish ships carried on the order of ~85,000 to 120,000 enslaved Africans across the Atlantic over roughly two centuries and ~340 voyages. The National Museum of Denmark cites ~120,000; the SlaveVoyages database records ~111,000 disembarked. A large share died on the Middle Passage; more died on the plantations.

Banned first — but kept going

The 1792 ban took effect in 1803, and illegal trading continued after. Denmark's African forts, no longer profitable, were abandoned or sold — Christiansborg and the rest went to Britain in 1850 for £10,000.

The numbers hide people: "~111,000 disembarked" already subtracts everyone who died at sea. Count the gap between embarked and disembarked and you are counting graves in the Atlantic.

Danish India: Tranquebar, Serampore, and the Nicobars

Denmark also held trading enclaves in India for more than two centuries — never very profitable, gradually squeezed out by the expanding British.

Tranquebar (1620–1845)

On the Tamil Nadu coast, Trankebar (today's Tharangambadi) was Denmark's main Indian outpost, anchored by Fort Dansborg, built in 1620 by the Danish East India Company. It was the base for the first Protestant missionaries in India.

Serampore / Frederiksnagore

Near Calcutta in Bengal, Serampore — Danish name Frederiksnagore — became known for its elegant houses as "the most beautiful European town in India," and a hub for early Bengali printing and translation.

The Nicobar Islands

Denmark claimed the Nicobar Islands in 1756 as Frederiksøerne, but disease and neglect defeated repeated attempts to settle them.

Sold off: Serampore went to Britain in 1839, Tranquebar and the mainland posts in 1845, and Denmark's rights in the Nicobars in 1868. The Indian chapter ended not in conquest but in a quiet series of sales.

Greenland: colonised in 1721, still unfinished

Greenland is the part of the empire that never ended. In 1721 the Dano-Norwegian Lutheran missionary Hans Egede — "the Apostle of Greenland" — sailed to convert the Inuit and founded a colony at Godthåb (Good Hope), today's Nuuk. Greenland stayed a Danish colony until 1953, and remains part of the Kingdom of Denmark today.

From colony to Self-Rule

Greenland went from colony (to 1953) to Home Rule (1979) to Self-Rule on 21 June 2009, which recognised Greenlanders as a distinct people, handed over control of natural resources, and set out a path to independence by referendum. Denmark still runs foreign affairs and defence and pays an annual block grant (~3.2 billion kroner).

The reckonings are recent

Two episodes are still being processed. The "little Danes" experiment (1951): 22 Inuit children, aged 5–10, were taken to Danish foster homes to be re-made as Danes; most were not orphans, half died young, and many never lived with their families again — Denmark apologised in 2022 (a first apology came in 2020). And the coil/"spiral" case: from the 1960s into the 1990s Danish doctors fitted IUDs in thousands of Greenlandic women and girls — some as young as 12 — often without consent, to hold down the birth rate; an official inquiry opened in 2023.

Don't file Greenland under "history": the sugar islands are gone, the Indian towns were sold, but Greenland is a live question — of self-determination, of apologies still being made, and of an island whose future its own people have not yet finally decided. The Faroe Islands, also Norse-settled and kept by Denmark after 1814, sit in the same category: an autonomous nation within the Kingdom, with home rule since 1948.

The shadow: what the empire cost

Told plainly, without the tidy-Denmark varnish. Every stat above is also a count of human lives spent to make sugar cheaper and a small kingdom richer.

The Middle Passage

Tens of thousands of the ~120,000 Africans forced onto Danish ships never reached land — the gap between the embarked and the disembarked is the death toll of the crossing. Those who survived were sold into plantation slavery on the islands.

Plantation slavery, then bound "free" labour

Enslaved people on St. Croix lived under a brutal code, revolted (the 1733 St. John rebellion; the 1848 rising), and, once freed, were bound again by labour ordinances until the 1878 Fireburn. Emancipation was extracted, not granted.

Greenland's open wounds

The "little Danes" children and the coil scandal are not distant history — the apologies and inquiries are happening now. Colonial harm in the Kingdom of Denmark is a present-tense subject, not a settled one.

Say it without flinching: Denmark being "first to ban the trade" and Denmark shipping ~100,000 people into slavery are the same country. The design shops and the slave forts share a flag. The honest move is to carry both facts at once.
