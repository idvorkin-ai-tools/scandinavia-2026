NETHERLANDS · EMPIRE

A small, wet country ran an empire on two oceans — and two centuries of slavery.

The VOC gets the famous story: spices, shares, the first stock market. But it was only half the machine. A second company took the Atlantic, a state took over the East Indies, and together they built New York, worked Caribbean and Surinamese plantations, forced Javanese peasants to grow Europe’s coffee, and shipped hundreds of thousands of enslaved Africans. In 2022 and 2023 a prime minister and a king finally apologised. Hold the Golden Age and the ledger of harm together — they are the same story.

The one-minute mental model

1. The VOC was only the east.

The famous company traded the Indian Ocean. A second firm, the West India Company, took the Atlantic — the Americas and West Africa — and that is where the transatlantic slave trade lived. (For spices, shares, and Banda, see the VOC page.)

2. The empire outlived both companies.

Both trading companies went bankrupt. But the Dutch state inherited their colonies — and the harshest chapters, the Cultivation System and the Aceh War, came under the government flag in the 1800s, not the corporate one.

3. It ended in a war they didn’t admit was one.

Indonesia declared independence in 1945; the Netherlands fought to keep it until 1949. In 2022 an official study called the Dutch conduct “structural” and “extreme violence.” The empire’s exit was as brutal as its middle.

Read it as one machine: the canal houses, the Rijksmuseum, the modern prosperity — and the plantations, the slave depots, the famines. Not two histories. One.

The Atlantic: a company for the other ocean

In 1621 the States General chartered the West India Company (Westindische Compagnie, WIC) to do in the Atlantic what the VOC did in Asia: raid the Spanish and Portuguese, and plant colonies. Its footprint became the parts of the map an American traveller knows best.

New Amsterdam → New York

The WIC settled Fort Orange (Albany) in 1624 and Manhattan in 1625–26 — the trading post of New Amsterdam, capital of the colony of New Netherland. In 1626 the director Peter Minuit “bought” Manhattan from the Lenape for goods later valued at 60 guilders (the origin of the “$24” myth; the Lenape did not share the Dutch idea of a permanent land sale). In 1664 an English fleet arrived; director Peter Stuyvesant surrendered without a fight, and New Amsterdam was renamed New York after the Duke of York.

Suriname, traded for New York

When the Second Anglo-Dutch War ended at the Treaty of Breda (1667), the Dutch kept the sugar colony of Suriname and let the English keep New Netherland. At the time it looked like a shrewd swap — a rich plantation coast for a cold northern town. Suriname became a brutal sugar-and-coffee slave economy; so many enslaved people escaped into the interior that they formed independent Maroon communities the Dutch never fully subdued.

Curaçao, the slave depot

The WIC seized Curaçao from Spain in 1634. Its deep harbour at Willemstad became, from the 1650s, one of the Caribbean’s great slave depots: enslaved Africans were landed, appraised by the pieza de Indias labour scale, and trans-shipped — often to Spanish American colonies under the asiento contract. Aruba, Bonaire, and the “SSS” islands filled out the Dutch Caribbean.

West Africa, the missing corner: the WIC took the castle of Elmina (in modern Ghana) from Portugal in 1637 and ran a string of forts on the Gold and Slave Coasts. This is where the Atlantic trade began for the people caught in it — the embarkation point of the crossing.

The slave trade, counted honestly

Here are the numbers, with the ranges historians actually use. Dutch ships carried roughly 550,000–600,000 enslaved Africans across the Atlantic — about 5–7% of the entire transatlantic trade — mostly between the 1630s and the early 1800s. Suriname and neighbouring Guyana were the largest destinations; Curaçao was the great transit hub.

Two different tallies — don’t merge them

This Atlantic figure (~550,000–600,000) is separate from the VOC’s enslavement in the Indian-Ocean world, where estimates run to hundreds of thousands and by some counts over a million people across two centuries (see the VOC page). Add them and you are looking at over a million human beings enslaved under the two Dutch flags — but they are counted in different oceans by different historians, so cite them as two ranges, not one headline.

Abolition came late — and slowly

The Netherlands abolished slavery in its colonies only in 1863 — decades after Britain (1833) and France (1848). Even then, the roughly 45,000 enslaved people in Suriname were forced to keep working the plantations for a ten-year transition, so real freedom came in 1873. Owners were compensated per enslaved person; the formerly enslaved received nothing. The date is now marked as Keti Koti — “the chain is broken.”

Say it plainly: Curaçao’s postcard harbour was an appraisal-and-resale point for human beings; Suriname’s plantation wealth flowed back up to Amsterdam. The 5–7% share sounds modest until you write it as a number: well over half a million people.

The East Indies after the VOC

When the bankrupt VOC was dissolved in 1799, the Dutch state took over its Asian territories as the Dutch East Indies — today’s Indonesia. The 19th century, under the government rather than the company, produced the empire’s most systematic extraction of all.

The Cultivation System (1830)

Facing bankruptcy after losing Belgium, Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch imposed the Cultuurstelsel in 1830. Javanese villages were forced to devote about a fifth of their land (or ~66 days of labour a year) to export crops — coffee, sugar, indigo, tobacco — delivered to the colonial government instead of paying tax. The profit, the batig slot, was shipped to the Netherlands, where in some years it funded a large share of the national budget and helped pay for Dutch railways and debt. It rescued Dutch finances; the bill was paid in Java.

Famine, and a famous protest

Because peasants grew indigo and sugar where they had grown rice, food failed. Famines struck Cirebon (1844) and Central Java — Demak and Grobogan — around 1849–50, killing large numbers and, in the worst districts, emptying whole populations. The outrage helped produce Max Havelaar (1860), the novel by “Multatuli” (Eduard Douwes Dekker) that indicted the system to Dutch readers. The forced-cultivation model was dismantled only gradually after 1870.

The Aceh War (1873–1914): the Dutch conquest of the sultanate of Aceh in northern Sumatra became their longest and bloodiest colonial war. By the historian Adrian Vickers’ estimate, 50,000–60,000 Acehnese died of violence and disease, with tens of thousands of Dutch and colonial (KNIL) soldiers and labourers dead as well — most of them from disease. Guerrilla resistance dragged on for decades.

The war they were slow to call a war

Days after Japan’s WWII surrender, on 17 August 1945, Sukarno and Hatta declared Indonesian independence. The Netherlands refused to accept it and fought for four years to reclaim the colony — euphemistically calling its two big offensives “police actions” (politionele acties) in 1947 and 1948.

“Structural extreme violence”

A major government-funded study by the KITLV, NIMH, and NIOD institutes — published as Beyond the Pale in 2022 — concluded that Dutch forces used “structural” and “extreme violence” as a matter of course, and that politicians, commanders, and the courts condoned and then covered it up. It overturned the decades-old official line of mere “excesses.”

The massacres have names

At Rawagede (West Java, December 1947) Dutch troops executed the village’s men — estimates run from about 150 to 431. In South Sulawesi (1946–47) the commando unit under Raymond Westerling killed at least ~1,500 people, with Indonesian estimates far higher. These were not rogue incidents but part of the pattern the 2022 study described.

The toll

Estimates of Indonesian deaths in the 1945–49 war run from roughly 97,000 to 100,000 and above once civilians are counted; about 4,000–5,000 Dutch military personnel died. The Netherlands transferred sovereignty at the Round Table Conference on 27 December 1949 — and only in 2023 officially accepted 1945 as the true date.

What’s left of the empire

The dismantling ran on for a generation after Indonesia. Some pieces became independent nations; others are still, constitutionally, part of the Kingdom of the Netherlands today.

Suriname, independent 1975

Suriname became a self-governing country within the Kingdom in 1954 and fully independent on 25 November 1975, leaving with a large Dutch aid package (some 3.5 billion guilders). A third of Surinamese people emigrated to the Netherlands around independence — one reason the Dutch–Surinamese and Afro-Caribbean communities are central to the country’s reckoning today.

The Caribbean, still in the Kingdom

The Kingdom of the Netherlands now has four constituent countries: the Netherlands, plus Aruba (since 1986) and Curaçao and Sint Maarten (since the 2010 break-up of the Netherlands Antilles). Three smaller islands — Bonaire, Sint Eustatius, and Saba, the “BES islands” — are special municipalities of the Netherlands proper. The empire’s Atlantic corner never fully left.

The reckoning: two apologies

For most of the 20th century the Netherlands told itself a gentle version of this story. That changed, publicly and at the top, only very recently.

The Prime Minister, December 2022

On 19 December 2022, at the National Archives in The Hague, PM Mark Rutte apologised on behalf of the Dutch state for its role in slavery — “to enslaved people in the past, everywhere in the world … and to all their descendants, up to the present day.” The government set aside a €200 million fund for awareness and addressing slavery’s ongoing effects.

The King, July 2023

On 1 July 2023 — Keti Koti, the 160th anniversary of the 1863 abolition — King Willem-Alexander went further, asking “forgiveness” for what he called a “crime against humanity,” and acknowledging that racism in Dutch society persists. A reigning monarch apologising for the crown’s own role in slavery was, for the Netherlands, unprecedented.

The honest close: the Golden Age you’ll admire in Amsterdam and the prosperity of the modern Netherlands are entangled with two centuries of colonial extraction — over half a million Africans shipped across the Atlantic, a forced-cultivation regime in Java, a slave depot in the Caribbean, and a violent war of departure. The country is, at last, starting to say so out loud. A good traveller can hold the beauty and the bill in the same hand.
