ICELAND · COLONIZED

Every other stop on this trip ran an empire. Iceland is the one that was ruled by one.

Amsterdam had the VOC. Denmark and Sweden carved up the Baltic. Norway sent out Vikings. Iceland is the exception on your itinerary: for roughly 682 years — from 1262 until 1944 — it was governed from somewhere else, first by Norway and then by Denmark. It is a rare thing in Europe, a nation that was on the receiving end of empire rather than the sending end. That history is exactly why its independence feels so fierce today.

Two capitals ruled this island — and neither was on it

The whole story is the distance on this map. The decisions that shaped Icelandic life — who could trade, which church was legal, whether famine relief would come — were made in Bergen and then Copenhagen, hundreds of sea-miles away, by people who had never set foot on the island. The one-minute mental model

1. Iceland was colonized, not a colonizer.

The Viking sagas are the famous part, but they are the prologue. After 1262 Iceland spent centuries as a poor, ruled-over dependency — taxed and administered from abroad, not a raiding power. On this trip it is the one that got ruled.

2. The rulers were far away — and that was the problem.

Distance wasn’t incidental; it was the whole grievance. Trade, church, law, and disaster relief were all decided by a court across the sea whose interests rarely matched the island’s.

3. Independence is recent, and hard-won.

The republic dates to 1944, not the Middle Ages. That is why the flag, the language, and the president carry so much weight here — the nation is younger than some of its grandparents.

Hold the nuance: Iceland is a colony-like case with an asymmetry. No native people were displaced when the Norse arrived — the settlers were themselves the kin of the crowns that later ruled them. So this is subordination and neglect, not conquest of an indigenous population. Say both plainly.

No one to displace: the empty-island exception

Almost every colonial story starts with a people already living on the land. Iceland’s does not. When Norse settlers began arriving around 874, archaeology and the sagas agree the island had no settled population — only, by tradition, a handful of Irish hermit monks (the papar) who are said to have left rather than live among heathens.

A settler society from the start

Estimates put the settlement wave (the Landnám, roughly 874–930) at some hundreds of initial settlers growing into thousands, mostly from western Norway and the Norse-Gaelic communities of the British Isles. They built a society from an empty slate.

Why the distinction matters

It means Iceland’s later colonization is the receiving-end kind — a European people ruled, taxed, and constrained by a distant crown — without the founding sin of dispossessing an indigenous nation. That is a genuinely unusual position, and worth naming precisely rather than flattening into either innocence or guilt.

How a free commonwealth signed itself away (1262–64)

From about 930 Iceland ran the Icelandic Commonwealth — a stateless order with no king, governed by chieftains (goðar) and a national assembly, the Alþingi, that met each summer at Þingvellir. It is one of the oldest surviving parliamentary traditions in the world.

The Sturlung Era breaks it

By the 13th century the Commonwealth had collapsed into a generation of feuding between a few over-mighty families — the Sturlung Era. Exhausted by civil war, and pressured by King Haakon IV of Norway (who had cultivated Icelandic chieftains for years), the leaders looked abroad for order.

The Old Covenant, 1262–64

In the Gamli sáttmáli (also called the Gissurarsáttmáli after the chieftain Gissur Þorvaldsson), the Icelanders agreed to pay tribute and accept the Norwegian king as sovereign in return for peace, guaranteed grain ships, and the keeping of their own laws. The free Commonwealth was over; foreign rule had begun — by agreement, then permanence.

From Norway to Denmark — inherited like property (1380/1397)

Iceland’s next change of ruler happened without a single Icelander being consulted. Through royal marriages and successions, the Norwegian crown — and everything attached to it — passed into a union dominated by Denmark.

1380: the crowns merge

With the death of King Olaf II, Norway (and with it Iceland, the Faroes, and Greenland) came under the Danish crown. Iceland moved to a new master purely as an appendage of Norway.

1397: the Kalmar Union

The union of Denmark, Norway, and Sweden was formalised at Kalmar. Iceland was now a distant dependency of a Copenhagen-centred realm — the arrangement, in Denmark’s case, that would last until the 20th century.

Benign neglect turns costly

Denmark had little use for Iceland’s fish and homespun wool, and trade and attention dwindled. Being ruled by a crown that found you unprofitable turned out to be its own kind of hardship.

The Reformation, imposed by beheading (1550)

When Denmark’s King Christian III converted his realm to Lutheranism, Iceland did not get a vote. The new state church was decreed from Copenhagen and pushed onto the island by royal authority — and the resistance ended at an executioner’s block.

Jón Arason holds out

Jón Arason, Catholic bishop of Hólar, was the last serious obstacle to the Danish Reformation in Iceland. He refused to accept Lutheranism and for a time defied the crown’s men outright — the last Icelander, in effect, to resist Copenhagen with force.

Beheaded at Skálholt, 7 Nov 1550

Jón Arason and his two sons, Ari and Björn, were captured and beheaded at Skálholt on 7 November 1550, despite appeals for mercy. With them died Catholic Iceland — and any pretence that the island’s faith was its own to choose. He is remembered today as a national and nationalist figure.

The monopoly that manufactured poverty (1602–1787)

The sharpest instrument of Danish rule was economic. In 1602 the crown handed all Icelandic trade to a closed circle of Danish merchants and forbade Icelanders from dealing with anyone else — the Einokunarverslunin, the Danish–Icelandic trade monopoly.

Trade by permission only

Foreign merchants were expelled; commerce was confined to 20–25 designated coastal posts run by licensed Danish traders at fixed prices. Those prices systematically undervalued Icelandic exports like dried fish and wool while inflating the cost of essentials such as grain and timber. An Icelander caught trading with an English or Dutch ship risked severe punishment.

Widely blamed for entrenched poverty

The monopoly is broadly held to have deepened Icelandic poverty for nearly two centuries. When the island’s first census was taken in 1703, the population was 50,366 and roughly a fifth were destitute. Historians debate how much of the misery was the monopoly versus climate, volcanism, and epidemics — but the monopoly is the part that was chosen in Copenhagen. It was loosened in 1787; full free trade with other nations did not arrive until 1855.

The shadow: when the sky rained fire and relief came slow (1783)

Here is the hardship of distant rule at its most literal. In June 1783 the Laki fissure (Lakagígar) began an eight-month eruption — the largest lava-and-gas outpouring in recorded history — and blanketed Iceland in a poisonous sulphuric haze.

Móðuharðindin — the Haze Hardships

Fluorine and sulphur poisoned the pastures. Roughly 80% of the island’s sheep and about half its cattle and horses died of fluorosis, and famine followed. Icelanders named the years that followed the Móðuharðindin, the Mist Hardships.

A fifth of the nation dies

Iceland’s population fell from 48,925 at the end of 1783 to 38,368 by the end of 1786 — on the order of 10,000 deaths, close to a fifth of everyone alive. It remains the worst catastrophe in the island’s history.

Ruled from far away

Denmark reportedly weighed evacuating the survivors to the Jutland heaths rather than sustaining them where they were. Whatever the mix of causes, the disaster played out under a government an ocean away — the lived reality of being someone else’s remote possession.

The comeback: a nation that talked its way to freedom

Iceland’s independence was won not by revolt but by decades of patient legal and cultural argument — and by the accident of a world war. Its architect was Jón Sigurðsson, the scholar-politician remembered as the father of Icelandic independence.

Step by step, 1843–1918

The Alþingi was restored as a consultative body in 1843; a constitution and limited self-rule came in 1874. In 1904 Home Rule moved the minister for Icelandic affairs from Copenhagen to Reykjavík — now required to speak Icelandic and answerable to the Icelandic parliament. The Act of Union of 1 December 1918 recognised a fully sovereign Kingdom of Iceland, joined to Denmark only through a shared king.

The republic, 17 June 1944

The Act of Union allowed either side to revisit it after 25 years. With Denmark under Nazi occupation and Iceland effectively cut off, Icelanders voted overwhelmingly — about 98% — to dissolve the union. The Republic of Iceland was proclaimed at Þingvellir on 17 June 1944, the date deliberately chosen as Jón Sigurðsson’s birthday. Independence declared while the former ruler was itself occupied.

Occupied one more time — this time to its benefit (1940–41)

There is a final irony. Iceland spent the war being occupied again — but by allies, and in a way that hastened its independence and its prosperity.

Operation Fork, 10 May 1940

After Germany overran Denmark, Britain feared the Nazis would seize strategically vital Iceland next. On 10 May 1940 Royal Marines landed at Reykjavík and occupied the neutral island without resistance. Iceland protested the violation of its neutrality — but the German alternative was worse.

The Americans take over, 1941

In July 1941 the still-officially-neutral United States relieved the British garrison, eventually stationing tens of thousands of troops. The occupation brought roads, an airport, and cash — an economic jolt that helped fund the modern, confident, independent Iceland that emerged in 1944.
