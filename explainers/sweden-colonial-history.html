SWEDEN · EMPIRE

Wait — Sweden had colonies?

The country you’re visiting for clean design and the Nordic model once planted a colony on the Delaware River, ran a slave-trading fort in Ghana, owned a Caribbean island for 94 years, and — at home — built the world’s first state race-biology institute. The neutral, progressive Sweden is real. So is this. Hold both.

One small crown, four uncomfortable places

Sweden’s empire never rivalled the Dutch or the British — but it was real, it spanned an ocean, and it reached from a fort in Ghana to an island in the Caribbean and a river in what’s now Delaware. The fourth marker is the one Swedes reckon with least: Sápmi, the Sámi homeland inside Sweden’s own north. Tap a marker.

The one-minute mental model

1. The empire was small — not innocent.

Sweden’s overseas holdings were modest and short-lived, and its slice of the Atlantic slave trade was minuscule beside Portugal or Britain. “Minor” is honest. “Clean” is not: the fort, the island, and the free port all touched slavery directly.

2. Gustavia was the real money.

The forgotten engine wasn’t plantations — it was neutrality-for-hire. Sweden ran Saint-Barthélemy as a tax-free port where other empires’ slave ships and cargoes moved freely. Profiting from the trade without owning many plantations is still profiting from the trade.

3. The hardest colony was internal.

The policies that shaped Sweden most weren’t across the ocean. They were aimed at the Sámi in the north, and at Swedes deemed “unfit” — nomad schools, land loss, race measurement, and sterilisation. Don’t file that under a separate heading; it’s the same century.

Read it as one story: the same era that gave the world Swedish “neutrality” and, later, the welfare state, also produced a race-biology institute and a sterilisation law. Progress and coercion shared a government.

New Sweden — a colony on the Delaware

In March 1638 two ships, the Kalmar Nyckel and the Fogel Grip, sailed into Delaware Bay under Peter Minuit — the same Minuit who had earlier directed the Dutch colony of New Netherland and, by legend, “bought” Manhattan. They built Fort Christina, named for Sweden’s child-queen, on the site of present-day Wilmington, Delaware.

Swedes, Finns, and log cabins

Over 17 years the colony drew roughly 600 settlers — mostly Swedes and Finns, plus Dutch, Germans, a Dane, and at least one Estonian. It never grew large, but it left a durable mark: historians credit New Sweden’s Finnish and Swedish settlers with introducing the American log cabin.

Swallowed by the Dutch

New Sweden lived by the sufferance of stronger neighbours. In 1655 the Dutch director-general Peter Stuyvesant sailed from New Amsterdam with a fleet, laid siege to Fort Christina, and forced its surrender. The colony was absorbed into New Netherland — which the English would take, and rename New York, less than a decade later.

The Swedish Gold Coast — a fort in the slave trade

This is the piece most travellers have never heard. In 1649 the financier Louis de Geer chartered the Swedish Africa Company; in April 1650 an expedition under Hendrik Carloff founded the Swedish Gold Coast on the Gulf of Guinea, in what is now Ghana. Its centrepiece was Fort Carlsborg (Carolusborg) at Cabo Corso — the site of today’s Cape Coast Castle, one of the most infamous slave-trading forts in West Africa.

What the fort was for

Gold and ivory were the pitch; people were part of the trade. During the Swedish period, enslaved Africans were shipped from the coast to the Portuguese sugar island of São Tomé, and by 1657 the company had contracted to deliver 500–600 enslaved people to the Dutch colony of Curaçao. Sweden held the fort only about a decade before losing it — to the Dutch again — in April 1663.

Small numbers, real complicity

Sweden’s direct traffic was tiny next to the roughly 12.5 million Africans forced across the Atlantic overall. But “small share” is not “no share.” A Swedish company built and ran a fort whose business included buying and selling human beings — and the same building later processed captives by the tens of thousands under other flags.

Saint-Barthélemy — the island and the free port

Sweden’s longest-held colony came by swap, not conquest. In 1784 France ceded the small Caribbean island of Saint-Barthélemy to King Gustav III in exchange for trading rights in Gothenburg. The Swedes renamed the harbour town Gustavia, after the king, and in 1785 declared it a free port — tax-free, open to all flags.

Neutrality as a business

The free port thrived precisely because Sweden stayed out of other powers’ wars. Ships came to trade duty-free; at its peak around 1,300 vessels called at Gustavia a year, and the town grew to roughly 5,000 people. The Swedish West India Company, chartered in 1786, ran the port and held exclusive commercial rights.

Slavery, on the books

The trade the port welcomed included the slave trade. A 1790 tariff made importing enslaved Africans duty-free. By an 1819 count, enslaved people made up roughly a third to half of the island’s population — somewhere between 1,283 and 2,033 people. Sweden abolished the island slave trade and, in the 1840s, slavery itself.

Sold back, quietly

The free port lost its edge, the economy sagged, and in 1878 Sweden sold Saint-Barthélemy back to France — after a local vote — for a modest sum. It was the last colony of the Swedish empire, closed with a bill of sale rather than a battle.

Say it plainly: Sweden didn’t need vast plantations to profit from slavery. Gustavia’s whole value proposition was to be the neutral, low-friction hub where an entire ocean’s trade — enslaved people included — could pass through and pay a fee.

The colony at home: Sápmi and the Sámi

Sweden’s heaviest and longest colonial project was internal. The Sámi — the Indigenous people of the European Arctic, whose homeland Sápmi spans northern Sweden, Norway, Finland, and Russia — were governed as subjects to be managed, not citizens to be included. The pattern is textbook settler colonialism, applied within one country’s own borders.

“Lapp shall remain Lapp”

Sweden’s official doctrine held that Sámi should stay reindeer-herders and stay separate. From 1913 the state ran segregated nomad schools for herding Sámi children — deliberately kept apart from Swedish schools and, in theory, from too much education — while the language and culture were pushed out. In the 1940s the huts became boarding schools; teaching was in Swedish.

Losing the land

Grazing lands, hunting and fishing rights, and traditional territory were steadily curtailed and redefined by the state — dispossession by legislation rather than by treaty. Reindeer husbandry was licensed and confined; Sámi who left herding could lose their recognised rights altogether.

The same story, three borders

Norway’s harsher fornorsking (“Norwegianisation”) and Finland’s assimilation ran in parallel — banned languages, boarding schools, suppressed religion. The Sámi story isn’t Sweden’s alone; each Nordic state now runs, or resists, its own truth-and-reconciliation reckoning.

The race-biology institute and the sterilisations

Here is the part hardest to square with the image. In 1922 Sweden opened the Statens institut för rasbiologi — the State Institute for Racial Biology — in Uppsala. It was the first state-funded race-science institute in the world. Under Herman Lundborg it measured skulls, catalogued bodies, and photographed populations — Sámi and Roma prominently among them — to chart and “protect” a supposed Nordic racial type.

From measurement to law

Race biology wasn’t a fringe hobby; it fed policy. In 1934 Sweden passed a sterilisation law, broadened in 1941. By the time it ended in 1975, roughly 63,000 people had been sterilised. A 2000 government commission estimated about 21,000 were forced and another ~6,000 coerced into a “voluntary” operation — the great majority of them women, many judged “feeble-minded,” “antisocial,” or of “mixed” descent.

Before, and alongside, the Nazis

Sweden’s eugenics program predated Nazi Germany’s and outlived it by three decades — running under the same Social Democratic governments that built the welfare state. The uncomfortable truth is that “folkhemmet,” the people’s home, and the sterilisation of the “unfit” were products of the same modernising, engineering impulse.

Don’t sanitise the timeline: the last forced-adjacent sterilisations in Sweden happened in 1975 — within living memory, not some distant colonial dawn. Survivors were still seeking compensation in the late 1990s and 2000s.

The Swedish West India Company is chartered to run the island; an 1790 tariff makes importing enslaved Africans duty-free. Gustavia booms as a neutral entrepôt.

1840s
