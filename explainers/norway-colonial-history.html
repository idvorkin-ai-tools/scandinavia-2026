NORWAY · EMPIRE?

Norway ran no empire of its own — so it helped run Denmark’s, then colonized its own north.

This is the country that hands out the Nobel Peace Prize and runs one of the world’s largest aid budgets per head. It is also a country that spent nearly three centuries as the junior half of a slave-trading kingdom, spent roughly a hundred years trying to erase the Sámi and Kven languages, and planted its flag on Antarctic ice and Arctic islands. All of that is Norway. The honest move is to hold it together.

The one-minute mental model

1. No empire of its own — but a partner in one.

Norway had almost no overseas colonies in its own name. What it had was a seat in the Danish crown: shipyards, timber, ports, sailors, and merchants that helped power Denmark’s Atlantic slave-and-sugar economy. Complicity, not command.

2. The real colony was internal.

The most sustained Norwegian colonial project pointed inward and north — a century-long campaign to dissolve the Sámi and Kven into a single Norwegian people. Empire didn’t need an ocean; it had Finnmark.

3. Then it drew lines around the poles.

In the 20th century, chasing whales and prestige, Norway annexed Arctic and Antarctic territory — Svalbard, Jan Mayen, Bouvetøya, Peter I Øy, Queen Maud Land. Small-nation imperialism, on ice.

Hold the two selves together: the Peace-Prize, aid-giving, mediator Norway is real. So is the slave-trade-adjacent, Sámi-assimilating, flag-planting Norway. Neither cancels the other; the country contains both.

Junior partner in a slave-trading kingdom

From the 1500s to 1814 Norway was the smaller kingdom in Denmark–Norway, governed from Copenhagen. That union owned a Caribbean colony — the Danish West Indies (St. Thomas from 1672, St. John, and St. Croix), sugar islands worked by enslaved Africans. Norway had no colony of its own there, but it was wired into the machine.

Norwegian ships & sailors

Norway was close to an equal partner in the dual monarchy’s Atlantic trade. Historians estimate roughly a third of the Danish-Norwegian slave tonnage was Norwegian, and Norwegian sailors crewed the voyages that carried enslaved people to the Caribbean.

Bergen & the merchant money

Bergen was by far the most important Norwegian seafaring city, and some of its merchants and notables profited from the trade. There is, as one Norwegian account puts it plainly, “much old money in the coastal cities founded on slavery.”

Timber that built the ships

Norwegian timber and shipbuilding fed the wider Atlantic economy, and eastern Norway’s timber merchants were the group most eager to loosen ties to Copenhagen — for their own trade, not out of anti-colonial principle.

Scale, honestly: across roughly 1660–1806, Denmark–Norway shipped more than 85,000 enslaved Africans; the fuller estimate for the whole trade is about 120,000. Denmark was the first state to ban the transatlantic slave trade (1792, effective 1803) — but voyages surged in the years before the ban bit, and Norwegian involvement in the wider trade continued after 1814.

1814, then 1905: how the map of Norway kept changing

Norway’s route out of the Danish union was not a war of independence — it was a chess move in someone else’s war. Because Denmark–Norway had backed Napoleon, the 1814 Treaty of Kiel handed Norway to Sweden as compensation. Denmark kept the overseas possessions — Greenland, Iceland, the Faroes, and the West Indies. Norwegians declared independence and wrote a constitution on 17 May 1814, but a short war and the Convention of Moss forced them into a personal union with Sweden instead.

A union, not a colony (1814–1905)

Under Sweden, Norway kept its own constitution, laws, parliament, army, navy, and treasury — only foreign policy ran through Stockholm. It was a junior partner again, but a self-governing one, not a subject territory.

Full independence (1905)

Norwegian nationalism and a fight over separate consular representation broke the union. A plebiscite on 13 August 1905 overwhelmingly backed dissolution; Sweden recognized Norway’s independence on 26 October, and Prince Carl of Denmark became King Haakon VII on 18 November. Only then was Norway a fully sovereign state.

Colonizer at home: Fornorsking

Here is the colonial story that actually belongs to Norway — and it is happening at the northern end of this very trip. Fornorsking, “Norwegianization,” was official state policy to assimilate the Sámi and later the Kven (descendants of Finnish-speaking migrants) into one ethnically and culturally uniform Norwegian population. It ran, in various forms, from around 1850 until the 1960s.

Schools built to erase a language

The policy was formalized from 1851, when the Storting set up a dedicated fund to finance it. Children were separated from families and sent to state boarding schools where only Norwegian was allowed; teachers were paid bonuses for effective Norwegianization, and pupils rewarded for switching languages fastest.

Bans and land loss

Minority languages were pushed out of schools and public life — a 1936 education law tightened the squeeze, with the Kven language especially targeted. Land and resource policy favored Norwegian speakers, eroding Sámi reindeer herding and Kven and Sea-Sámi fisheries.

Nationalism, dressed as progress

The drive mixed 19th-century nationalism with religious hierarchy — a Lutheran state church over Sámi spiritual traditions — and social-Darwinist ideas of a “higher” culture absorbing a “lower” one. It was framed as civilizing, which is exactly how colonial assimilation is usually sold.

Don’t soften it: this was a democratic, law-abiding state deliberately trying to dissolve two peoples’ languages and identities — for over a century, on its own soil. Same logic as overseas colonialism, pointed inward.

The reckoning: a Truth and Reconciliation Commission

Norway is now doing something many former colonial powers have not: examining the record officially. In 2018 the Storting established a Truth and Reconciliation Commission to investigate the Norwegianization policy and injustice against the Sámi and Kven (and, later, the Forest Finns). It was chaired by former minister Dagfinn Høybråten.

The report

After five years of testimony and research, the commission delivered a roughly 700-page report to the Storting on 1 June 2023. It documented not only the historical policy but its lingering effects — on language, health, culture, and traditional livelihoods — persisting well past the policy’s official end.

What followed

In 2024 the Storting responded with a set of resolutions and a formal apology to the Sámi, Kven/Norwegian Finns, and Forest Finns. Sámi voices have stressed that recognition is a beginning, not a settlement — the harder work of restitution, language revival, and land rights is ongoing.

Flags on the ice: Norway’s polar empire

The other Norwegian imperialism is easy to miss because it’s mostly uninhabited rock and ice. In the interwar decades Norway annexed a string of Arctic and Antarctic territories — driven above all by whaling interests wanting harbors, sovereignty, and prestige.

Svalbard (1920)

The Svalbard Treaty, signed in Paris on 9 February 1920 (in force 1925), gave Norway full sovereignty over the archipelago — but with strings: citizens of all signatory states get equal rights of access and economic activity, and the islands are demilitarized. Sovereignty with an asterisk.

Jan Mayen & Bouvetøya

Norway annexed the Arctic volcanic island of Jan Mayen (1929/1930) and the sub-Antarctic Bouvet Island — flag raised 1 December 1927, royal decree 23 January 1928 — the latter financed by whaling magnate Lars Christensen. Bouvetøya is Norway’s only fully sovereign dependency.

Peter I Øy & Queen Maud Land

Norwegian expeditions claimed Peter I Island (1929, annexed 1931) and the vast Queen Maud Land sector of Antarctica (claimed 1939), stretching from 20°W to 45°E. These claims are now frozen under the Antarctic Treaty, but Norway still asserts them.

Whaling was the engine: whaling concerns were the main reason for the Antarctic and sub-Antarctic annexations of the interwar years — Bouvetøya (1928), Peter I Øy (1931), and Dronning Maud Land (1939). This was empire measured in barrels of whale oil.

The whaling empire that drew the map

By the 1920s and 1930s Norway was the world’s preeminent whaling nation. Norwegian crews pioneered pelagic whaling — factory ships that processed whales at sea, far from any land station — after experiments starting around 1904. That year, Norwegian captain C.A. Larsen opened the first modern Antarctic whaling station at Grytviken, South Georgia (under a British lease), and Norwegians dominated the Southern Ocean for decades after.

Industrial scale

By 1930 the Antarctic hunt ran on roughly 41 factory ships and 232 catcher boats plus six shore stations — most of the crews Norwegian. It made fortunes (and men like Lars Christensen), funded the polar annexations, and helped push several whale populations toward collapse.

Why it belongs in this story

The whaling empire is the thread that ties the polar claims together: the territories were staked to serve the fleet. It’s a reminder that Norwegian expansion in the 20th century was resource extraction with a flag on top — the same shape as older empires, in a colder ocean.
