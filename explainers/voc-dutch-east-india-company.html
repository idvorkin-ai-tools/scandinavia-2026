AMSTERDAM · EMPIRE

One Amsterdam company invented the stock market — then ran an empire from a boardroom.

The Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC) is the reason there is an Amsterdam Stock Exchange, the reason “shareholder” is a word, and one of the reasons the canal houses are so gorgeous. It is also the reason an entire island people almost ceased to exist. Both facts are true at once — hold them together.

The Dutch States General chartered the VOC on 20 March 1602 with a 21-year monopoly on all Dutch trade east of the Cape of Good Hope.

Roughly 1,100 ordinary investors — merchants, but also servants and small savers — subscribed nearly 6.5 million guilders in the world’s first public share offering.

Ruled by
17

The “Heren XVII” — the Lords Seventeen, eight of them from Amsterdam — governed the whole enterprise like a corporate board.

Dissolved
1799

After roughly 197 years the debt-laden company was wound up on 31 December 1799 and its territories were nationalised by the Dutch state.

The whole point of the VOC is the distance on this map. The Heren XVII met in Amsterdam — a couple of canals from your hotel — and issued orders that moved fleets, built forts, and seized islands from the Cape of Good Hope to the Spice Islands. Tap a marker.

The one-minute mental model

1. It was a company with a country’s powers.

The VOC could raise armies, fight wars, sign treaties, mint coins, and found colonies — powers that normally belong only to states. It was a corporation wearing a government’s clothes.

2. It invented tools you still use.

Public shares, an IPO, a secondary market where anyone could buy and sell — the modern stock market was prototyped here in 1602. When you own an index fund, you are using the VOC’s idea.

3. The wealth had a body count.

The same monopoly that funded the Golden Age was enforced with conquest, forced labor, and slavery. Do not launder that into “clever old merchants.”

Read it as one machine: the financial genius and the violence were not separate departments. The tradable share existed to fund voyages; the voyages seized the monopoly; the monopoly paid the dividend.

The firsts that still shape your wallet

The first tradable public shares

On 20 March 1602 the VOC opened its books to any citizen of the Dutch Republic — no minimum investment, no aristocratic gatekeeping. About 1,100 people bought in, raising close to 6.5 million guilders. Crucially, those shares were transferable: you could sell yours to someone else without dissolving the company.

The first modern stock market

Because the shares could change hands, a secondary market sprang up almost immediately — traders buying and reselling VOC stock in the open air and, from 1611, in a purpose-built exchange building. That market became the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, widely called the world’s first modern stock market.

Why it matters: the joint-stock company with freely tradable shares became the template for large enterprise worldwide. The IPO, the shareholder, the ticker-less trading floor — all rehearsed here first.

A state wearing a company’s clothes

Read the 1602 charter closely and it stops looking like a business license. The Dutch government handed the VOC powers usually reserved for sovereign nations.

Wage war & keep armies

The VOC maintained its own private army and navy and was legally authorised to wage war against rivals and local rulers in Asia.

Sign treaties & mint coins

It negotiated treaties with foreign powers as an equal, struck its own coinage for circulation across its Asian territories, and ran its own courts.

Plant colonies

It founded and governed settlements — most famously Batavia (today’s Jakarta) and a refreshment station at the Cape of Good Hope — as a corporate empire.

The Heren XVII: all of this answered to a 17-seat board — eight delegates from the Amsterdam chamber, four from Zeeland, the rest from smaller chambers, with the seventeenth seat rotated so Amsterdam could never quite outvote everyone. A boardroom governing colonies half a world away.

Why spices were worth more than gold

The engine underneath all of it was a spice monopoly — above all nutmeg, mace (the lacy husk around the nutmeg seed), and cloves. In 1600s Europe these preserved food, flavored it, signaled wealth, and were even believed to ward off plague. And nutmeg and mace grew, at the time, in essentially one place on Earth: the tiny Banda Islands.

A monopoly on a rock

Because the trees grew nowhere else, whoever controlled the Bandas controlled the entire world supply. A sack of nutmeg bought in the islands could be sold in Amsterdam for a fortune — margins that made spices, pound for pound, more valuable than gold.

The most valuable company ever?

You’ll see the VOC called the most valuable company in history — figures like $7–10 trillion in “today’s money” get thrown around. Treat those with heavy skepticism: they come from compounding an assumed inflation rate across 300+ years, and small errors explode. Its real peak market value was roughly 78 million guilders. Astonishing for its day; not literally ten Apples.

The shadow: what the monopoly cost

Here is the part the postcard leaves out, told plainly. A monopoly on a spice that grows in one place is only worth something if you can stop everyone else from trading it — and the VOC was willing to enforce that with extermination.

Banda, 1621

When the Bandanese kept trading nutmeg with other buyers, governor-general Jan Pieterszoon Coen led a conquest that culminated in 1621. Estimates hold that more than 90% of the roughly 15,000 Bandanese died — killed, deported, or lost to the starvation and disease that followed. A group of village leaders was publicly executed and beheaded. The survivors, about a thousand people, were shipped to Batavia.

Forced labor replaces a people

With the islanders gone, the VOC handed the emptied nutmeg groves to Dutch perkeniers (planters) who worked them with enslaved and forced laborers. The monopoly was now secured not by trade but by depopulation and coercion.

Entangled with slavery

This was not an aberration. Across two centuries historians estimate the VOC bought, traded, and used somewhere between roughly 600,000 and over 1 million enslaved people — building forts, working households, and laboring in settlements from Batavia to the Cape of Good Hope.

Say it without flinching: the Banda massacre was not collateral damage. It was a business decision to protect a monopoly — the same monopoly that paid the dividends that helped build the canal houses you’ll be admiring.

Timeline: what happened here

20 Mar 1602

The States General charter the VOC, merging rival Dutch trading companies and granting a 21-year Asian monopoly. Same day: the first public share offering.

1602–1611

A secondary market in VOC shares emerges in Amsterdam; a dedicated exchange building opens in 1611 — the seed of the world’s first modern stock market.

1609–1621

The VOC fights a prolonged campaign to seize the nutmeg-and-mace Banda Islands.

1619

Jan Pieterszoon Coen founds Batavia (modern Jakarta) as the VOC’s fortified Asian headquarters.

1621

The conquest of Banda culminates in massacre and deportation; over 90% of the Bandanese population is destroyed, locking in the nutmeg monopoly.

1652

The VOC establishes a refreshment station at the Cape of Good Hope — a colony that would grow a large enslaved population.

Later 1600s

Peak wealth: the spice monopoly, Golden-Age Amsterdam, and dividends that dazzle Europe.

1700s

Decline: corruption, rising competition, war losses, and mounting debt hollow the company out.

31 Dec 1799

The VOC is dissolved, ~12 million guilders in debt; the Dutch state takes over its territories as the Dutch East Indies.
