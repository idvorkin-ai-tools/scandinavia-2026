Two short nights to kick off the trip — base near Reykjavík and aim the daylight at waterfalls, craters, and a lava cave.

The nature stuff is the headline, but with only two nights you won't get all of it — pick a direction and commit. Below: the waterfalls and wild places worth chasing, the little town to call home, and the one big underground outing. July daylight stretches past 20 hours, so you've got room to be greedy.

WATERFALLS & NATURE

Brúarfoss

The bluest waterfall in Iceland — a glacial-melt turquoise that doesn't quite look real. It's about 90–120 minutes from Reykjavík on the Brúará river, and since the official lot opened in 2023 it's just a 5–10 minute walk in from the parking. (The old long trail is a 90-minute round trip past two smaller falls, Hlauptungufoss and Miðfoss, if you want the full version.)

This one slots in beautifully between the Geysir geothermal area and Laugarvatn/Þingvellir, so string it into a Golden Circle loop rather than treating it as a standalone drive.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · small parking fee (~750 ISK) · verify before you go

Goðafoss

The "waterfall of the gods" — a wide, horseshoe-shaped curtain in North Iceland, right off the Ring Road between Akureyri and Mývatn. It's an easy stop with viewing platforms on both banks, so you can see it from a couple of angles for the cost of a short walk.

Worth knowing: this is a long way from your Reykjavík base (north side of the island), so it really only makes sense if your two nights swing a bigger loop. Otherwise file it as aspirational.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · verify before you go

Skógafoss

The classic broad south-coast waterfall — a 60-meter wall of water you can walk right up to (expect spray and rainbows on a sunny day). There's a staircase to a top viewpoint if your legs are willing, and it sits directly on Route 1, so it's one of the most painless big falls to reach.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · verify before you go

Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon

A serpentine, moss-walled gorge carved into the rock ~9,000 years ago, up to 100 meters deep with the Fjaðrá river snaking along the floor. It's in South Iceland off Route 1 near Kirkjubæjarklaustur; the rim walk is an easy, family-friendly 45–60 minutes with dramatic viewpoints the whole way.

Stay on the marked rim trail — descending into the canyon isn't allowed (fragile vegetation), and it occasionally closes for trail protection. Check ust.is or safetravel.is if you want to confirm access before driving out.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · lower-lot parking ~1,000 ISK · verify before you go

Diamond Beach

Chunks of glacier ice scattered across black volcanic sand — they break off the Jökulsárlón glacier lagoon, drift to the sea, and wash back up to glitter on the beach. It's on the south coast next to the lagoon, so pair the two: lagoon on one side of the road, beach on the other.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · verify before you go

Víti Crater

A volcanic crater lake with milky, mineral-blue water. Heads up: there are two Vítis. The accessible one is Víti in Krafla (North Iceland, near Mývatn) — it sits beside a paved road, formed in 1724. The other, Víti in Askja, is deep in the Central Highlands and needs an F-road drive (and sometimes lets you swim). For a two-night trip, the Krafla one is the realistic pick.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · Krafla open in summer; Askja highland roads are F-road only · verify before you go

Valley of Tears (Sigöldugljúfur)

A scenic detour: a canyon walled in vivid green moss with dozens of slim waterfalls spilling down it in a row — hence "valley of tears." It's in the Highlands of South Iceland near the Sigalda power station, off Road 208, so it's more of a deliberate side-quest than a drive-by.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (daylight best) · Road 208 / highland access · verify before you go
TOWN & FOOD

Hafnarfjörður (home base)

The little town about 15 minutes outside Reykjavík — quieter, harbor-side, and a good place to actually sleep between long daylight drives. Use it as the base for the leg; everything below is within easy reach, and there's a swim spot, a pond, and the seaside all close at hand for a slow morning.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Open access (it's a town) · verify before you go

Café Barbara

The coffee stop in Hafnarfjörður — a cozy community café on Strandgata that shifts character through the day: fragrant coffee and cakes in the morning, sandwiches at lunch, a glass of wine or a cocktail by evening. Good for the start of a drive or a quiet reset after one.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: opens ~09:00 Mon–Fri, ~10:00 weekends; closing varies — check @barbarakaffibar · verify before you go

Sól Restaurant

Dinner inside an actual greenhouse — a striking glass-walled room in Hafnarfjörður where you eat above a living, growing harvest. It's farm-to-table New Nordic, with a lot of the produce grown on-site. This is the splurge/special-occasion meal of the leg, so book ahead.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: Wed–Sun 17:30–21:30; closed Mon–Tue · reservations recommended · verify before you go

Icelandic street food & the hot-dog stand

Graze between stops — and the icon is the hot dog. Bæjarins Beztu Pylsur by the old Reykjavík harbor (Tryggvagata 1) has been slinging lamb-based hot dogs since 1937; order one "with everything" (eina með öllu) and you've completed a rite of passage. Cheap, fast, and open late — the perfect fuel between long daylight drives.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: roughly 10:00–01:00 (later on weekends), but locations vary · verify before you go
CAVES & TOURS

Raufarhólshellir lava caves

The one big underground outing — a guided walk through one of Iceland's longest lava tunnels, about 30 minutes from Reykjavík with pickup available right in the city. The standard tour runs roughly 1 hour; if you want the deep "Lava Falls" version it's a fitter 3–4 hours. Tours start hourly through the day.

Igor's note holds: it's cold and drippy down there, so bring warm and waterproof layers plus a hat. Book a slot in advance, especially in July.

More details ↗🕑 Hours: tours hourly, morning to late afternoon (~50–60 min standard); winter reduced · verify before you go
